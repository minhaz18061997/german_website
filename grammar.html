📖 Grammar

Only the grammar rules that appear in 80% of German sentences. Master these before moving to anything else.

A1 Grammar — 8 rules
TopicRuleExample (DE)Example (EN)Common MistakePro Tip
Articles (der/die/das)Nouns have 3 genders: der (m), die (f), das (n). Always learn the article with the noun.der Mann, die Frau, das Kindthe man, the woman, the childForgetting the genderLearn nouns as 'der/die/das + word', never just the word alone
Plural formsGerman plurals are irregular – must memorise. Common patterns: -e, -er, -en, -(e)s, -das Buch → die Bücherthe book → the booksAdding -s to everything (English habit)Group nouns by plural pattern as you learn them
Personal Pronounsich, du, er/sie/es, wir, ihr, sie/Sie – must match verb endingich bin, du bist, er istI am, you are, he isMixing du (informal) and Sie (formal)Use 'du' with friends, 'Sie' with strangers/elders
Verb Conjugation (Present)Regular verbs: remove -en, add endings: -e, -st, -t, -en, -t, -enich spiele, du spielst, er spieltI play, you play, he playsUsing infinitive as conjugated formStem-changers (fahren→fährst) need separate memorisation
Irregular Verbs (sein/haben)sein (to be): bin/bist/ist/sind/seid/sind. haben: habe/hast/hat/haben/habt/habenIch bin müde. Er hat Hunger.I am tired. He is hungry.Using 'haben' instead of 'sein' and vice versaThese two verbs appear in almost every sentence – memorise first!
Basic Word Order (SVO)Main clause: Subject → Verb → Object. Verb is always in 2nd position!Ich kaufe ein Buch.I buy a book.Putting verb at end in main clauseThe verb-2nd rule is the #1 German rule
Negation with 'nicht' & 'kein'kein = no/not a (before nouns). nicht = not (before adjectives/verbs/adverbs)Ich habe kein Geld. Ich bin nicht müde.I have no money. I am not tired.Using 'nicht' before all nounskein replaces ein: ein Buch → kein Buch
Question FormationYes/No: Verb first. W-questions: W-word → Verb → SubjectHast du Zeit? / Wo bist du?Do you have time? / Where are you?Forgetting to invert verb and subjectW-words: Wer, Was, Wo, Wann, Warum, Wie, Welch-
A2 Grammar — 8 rules
TopicRuleExample (DE)Example (EN)Common MistakePro Tip
Accusative CaseDirect object uses accusative. Der → den (masculine only changes!)Ich sehe den Mann.I see the man.Forgetting der→den for masculineOnly masculine changes in accusative: der→den, ein→einen
Dative CaseIndirect object / after dative prepositions. Der→dem, Die→der, Das→demIch gebe dem Mann das Buch.I give the man the book.Mixing accusative and dativeDative prepositions: mit, nach, bei, von, aus, seit, zu, gegenüber
Accusative PrepositionsAlways take accusative: durch, für, gegen, ohne, umDas Geschenk ist für dich.The gift is for you.Using dative after these prepositionsDOGFU: Durch, Ohne, Gegen, Für, Um
Two-way Prepositionsan, auf, hinter, in, neben, über, unter, vor, zwischen + Acc (motion) or Dat (location)Ich lege es auf den Tisch. Es liegt auf dem Tisch.I put it on the table. It is on the table.Always using one or the otherMotion=Accusative (Wohin?), Location=Dative (Wo?)
Modal Verbs6 modals: können, müssen, dürfen, wollen, sollen, mögen. Conjugated + infinitive at END.Ich kann heute nicht kommen.I cannot come today.Putting infinitive in middle of sentenceInfinitive always goes to the very end!
Separable VerbsPrefix separates in main clause and goes to END. anrufen→ Ich rufe dich an.Er macht das Licht aus.He turns off the light.Forgetting to separate the prefixPrefixes: an-, ab-, auf-, aus-, ein-, mit-, vor-, zu-
Perfect Tense (Perfekt)Most common past tense in spoken German. haben/sein + past participle (Partizip II) at end.Ich habe gegessen. Ich bin gegangen.I ate. I went.Using haben with motion/state verbsMotion/state change verbs use SEIN: gehen, fahren, kommen, werden
Adjective EndingsAdjectives before nouns take endings that depend on gender, case, and article typeDas ist ein kleiner Hund.That is a small dog.Using no ending on adjectivesLearn the 3-pattern table: after der/ein/no article
B1 Grammar — 7 rules
TopicRuleExample (DE)Example (EN)Common MistakePro Tip
Subordinate ClausesWith subordinating conjunctions, verb goes to END of clauseIch lerne Deutsch, weil es wichtig ist.I learn German because it is important.Not sending verb to endConjunctions: weil, dass, wenn, obwohl, damit, als, ob
Relative ClausesRelative pronoun (der/die/das) agrees with noun in gender; verb goes to endDas ist das Buch, das ich lese.That is the book that I am reading.Wrong gender of relative pronounRelative pronoun = same gender as noun it refers to
Konjunktiv II (Subjunctive)Used for polite requests, hypotheticals. würde + infinitive OR special formIch würde gern kommen. / Ich hätte Zeit.I would like to come. / I would have time.Using indicative for polite requestsHätte, wäre, könnte, müsste, dürfte are the key forms
Genitive CaseShows possession. -(e)s added to masculine/neuter nouns. von + dative is informal alternative.Das Auto meines Vaters.My father's car.Using apostrophe-s like EnglishGerman genitive has NO apostrophe: Vaters not Vater's
Passive Voicewerden + Partizip II. Expresses action without naming doer.Das Buch wird gelesen.The book is being read.Confusing with Perfekt (haben/sein + PP)Passive = werden + PP; Perfect = haben/sein + PP
Reflexive VerbsMany German verbs are reflexive (with sich). Reflexive pronoun must match subject.Ich freue mich. / Du freust dich.I am happy. / You are happy.Forgetting the reflexive pronounCommon ones: sich freuen, sich vorstellen, sich setzen, sich fühlen
Infinitive with 'zu'After certain verbs/phrases, infinitive needs 'zu'. With separable verbs, zu goes in middle.Ich versuche, Deutsch zu lernen.I am trying to learn German.Forgetting 'zu' or placing it wrongNo zu after modals! Ich muss lernen (not: zu lernen)

Extended Participial PhrasesParticiple phrase replaces relative clause in formal/written Germandas von mir gelesene Buchthe book read by meTrying to use in speech (too complex)Passive only in written German – use relative clauses when speaking

Double InfinitiveModal + past infinitive creates double infinitive at endIch habe kommen müssen.I had to come.Putting participle instead of infinitiveDouble infinitive: habe + kommen + müssen (both infinitives!)
